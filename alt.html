The Framework

Before the
Commitment,
the Investigation.

The most expensive mistake in career preparation is committing significant time and money before knowing if the path is actually right. ALT is designed to make investigation come first — and exploration feel possible.

Start the Finder
01
Identify What Pulls You
Not a personality type. Not a test score. What actually interests you, and why? The Dispatch, the quiz, and the pathway stories are designed to help surface real interests — not match you to whatever's popular right now.
02
Get a Firsthand View
Meet people who are already doing it. The community voices section is filled with real people in real fields. The resource organizations offer job shadow programs, open houses, and mentorships that give you actual exposure before any commitment.
03
Eliminate What Doesn't Fit
Crossing a path off the list is not failure — it's progress. The goal isn't to find the one perfect path. It's to narrow from many possibilities down to the few worth exploring deeply. That's the job of investigation.
04
Follow Up Before You Commit
Expert advisement. Workplace observation. Informational interviews. The organizations listed in ALT can connect you with field contacts who can answer the questions no website can. Then — and only then — commit time and money.

For Parents

The Questions
You're
Actually
Asking

You want your kid to be secure. That's the whole thing. Here's what the research actually says about alternative career paths — without the guilt trip about college, and without the oversell on trades.

Is a trade career actually stable long-term? +
Yes — and increasingly more so than many white-collar fields. AI is automating knowledge work faster than physical skilled trades. An electrician, HVAC tech, or plumber cannot be outsourced or replaced by software. Additionally, trade workers can transition into business ownership (often by year 6–8), dramatically increasing their earning ceiling. The data consistently shows trade workers out-earn most college graduates within a decade — and carry significantly less debt.
What if my child changes their mind after starting a program? +
Most alternative pathways are designed for exactly this. A 6-month welding certification costs a fraction of a semester of college. An HVAC certification can be completed in 18 months. These shorter commitments mean the cost of changing direction is far lower than it would be after two years at a four-year university. The framework ALT recommends: investigate first, commit second, and treat exploration as the goal — not the failure.
How do I help my child figure out what they actually want? +
The most useful thing you can do is get them exposure before asking for commitment. Job shadows, open house events at JCTC or IEC Kentuckiana, informational interviews with working professionals in a field — these experiences are more valuable than any personality test. ALT's Finder quiz is a starting point for conversation, not a verdict. Use it to open doors, not close them.
What about college — is it completely off the table? +
Not at all. ALT isn't anti-college. It's pro-intention. If your child has a specific, concrete reason for a four-year degree — a field that genuinely requires it, a specific institution with a specific program, a clear career goal that demands it — then college makes complete sense. What ALT pushes back on is college as the default answer when no other options have been explored. The question isn't "college or not?" — it's "what path is actually right, and have we looked at all of them?"
How do I find a mentor or advisor in a specific field? +
The organizations listed in ALT — ACE Mentor, IBEW, IEC, KY Career Center — all have direct contact programs. ACE Mentor specifically connects high school students with working professionals in architecture, construction, and engineering at no cost. The Contribute section of ALT also lets you submit a request for specific connections — we'll route it to the right people in the community.
